## A 200-year-old question

In the 1840s, a mathematician named **Ada Lovelace** wrote the first computer program — for a machine that was never even built. She realized something profound: a computer doesn't just *do* a calculation, it does it by following **steps**, and the *number* of steps is what really matters.

A hundred years later, as real computers appeared, scientists kept bumping into the same wall. A program that worked fine on ten things would **choke** on ten thousand. The machine wasn't broken — the *method* simply took too many steps. So they invented a language to talk about it: **Big-O notation**. It doesn't measure seconds. It measures **how the number of steps grows as the job gets bigger**.

That single idea is the lens you've been looking through this entire class.

## What you've actually learned

Think back over the shapes you drew with your own code:

| Shape | Name | You met it in… |
|-------|------|----------------|
| flat line | **O(1)** | a hash-table lookup that jumps straight to the answer |
| barely rises | **O(log n)** | binary search; walking one path down a search tree |
| straight climb | **O(n)** | linear search, checking every box |
| steep hill | **O(n²)** | bubble sort, swapping over and over |
| gentle dive | **O(n log n)** | merge sort, divide and conquer |

You didn't just *read* about these — you wrote a step-counter, returned the number, and watched the curve bend. That's the whole game: **know the shape your code draws, and pick the flatter one.**

## It's hiding in every class

Big-O isn't only here. It's everywhere you've been:

- **Networks** — DNS *caching* means the second time you visit a site, the slow lookup is skipped. That's turning an O(n) trip into an O(1) one.
- **White Hat** — every extra digit you add to a password multiplies a hacker's work by **ten**. That explosion is exactly why long passwords are safe.
- **Operating Systems** — the scheduler is constantly choosing *whose turn is next*, using the queues you now understand from the inside.
