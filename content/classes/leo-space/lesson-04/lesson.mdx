## Big Idea: A Tug-of-War of Energy

Whether you fall back or fly away comes down to a contest between two energies.

**Kinetic energy** — the energy of *moving*. The faster you go, the more you have. Per kilogram, it's:

$$
KE = \tfrac{1}{2} v^2
$$

**Potential energy** — the energy gravity has "stored up" by pulling on you. Here's the strange part: near a planet, gravitational potential energy is **negative**, and it's *most* negative when you're close. Per kilogram:

$$
PE = -\frac{G M}{r}
$$

Think of the planet as the bottom of a deep well. Down close, you're stuck deep in the well (very negative PE). To get out, you have to "pay" your way up — and your kinetic energy is the only money you've got.

> **Why negative?** It's just a bookkeeping choice: we say PE = 0 infinitely far away (totally free of the planet), so anywhere *closer* — still trapped by gravity — counts as below zero. The deeper in the well, the more negative.

---

## The Number That Decides Everything

Add the two together and you get the **total energy** (per kilogram):

$$
E = \tfrac{1}{2}v^2 - \frac{G M}{r}
$$

This one number tells you the whole story, because total energy never changes as you coast — gravity just keeps trading kinetic for potential and back. So whatever $E$ is at launch, that's your destiny:

- **$E < 0$** → you're **bound**. You don't have enough kinetic energy to climb out of the well. You'll fall back or settle into an orbit. (A circular orbit always has $E < 0$.)
- **$E = 0$** → you have *exactly* enough to barely escape, coasting to a stop infinitely far away.
- **$E > 0$** → you **escape** with energy to spare and never come back.

So predicting a launch is as easy as checking one sign: **is $E$ negative or not?**

---

## Escape Speed

The magic moment is $E = 0$ — the slowest speed that still lets you escape. Set total energy to zero and solve for $v$:

$$
\tfrac{1}{2}v^2 - \frac{G M}{r} = 0
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
v_{esc} = \sqrt{\frac{2 G M}{r}}
$$

That's the **escape speed**. Go any faster and you're free; any slower and the planet reels you back in.

Notice it's the circular orbital speed from Lesson 2 times $\sqrt{2}$:

$$
v_{esc} = \sqrt{2}\; v_{circle} \approx 1.41 \times v_{circle}
$$

So to *escape* a planet you only need about **41% more speed** than to *orbit* it. Some real numbers:

- **Earth's surface:** about $11{,}186$ m/s — that's **40,000 km/h**, the famous "11.2 km/s." Every rocket that's ever left Earth had to beat this.
- **The Moon:** only about $2{,}375$ m/s — which is exactly why a small lander could blast off the Moon and come home, but you need a giant rocket to leave Earth.

---

## Predicting a Launch in Code

You don't need a simulation for this — just the energy formula and a comparison. Compute the total energy and check its sign:

```python
def will_escape(M, v, r):
    energy = 0.5 * v**2 - G * M / r
    return energy >= 0
```

If `energy` is zero or positive, the object escapes — return `True`. If it's negative, gravity wins — return `False`. One formula, one comparison, and you can predict the fate of anything you launch from anywhere.

> **It works on the whole universe.** This same check tells you whether a comet will swing past the Sun once and leave forever, or come back in a few thousand years. Whether a moon stays put. Whether a probe reaches deep space. One sign, the whole story.
