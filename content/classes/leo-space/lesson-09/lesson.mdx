## For thousands of years, the sky was a separate world

Ancient people noticed something that seemed *obvious*: things on Earth **fall down**, but the Moon and stars **don't.** They float up there, going around and around, never dropping. So everyone agreed there must be **two sets of rules** — earthly rules for falling rocks, and heavenly rules for the perfect, floating sky. For almost 2,000 years, that's what the smartest people believed.

They were wrong. And it took some careful watchers to prove it.

---

## Kepler: the orbits aren't even circles (early 1600s)

A German astronomer named **Johannes Kepler** got his hands on years of incredibly careful measurements of the planets (made by his boss, Tycho Brahe). Kepler stared at the numbers for years and discovered something shocking: the planets don't go in perfect circles. They go in **ellipses** — slightly stretched ovals — moving faster when they're closer to the Sun and slower when far. You found the same thing when you stretched your orbits into ellipses and saw perigee and apogee. Kepler found the *shape* of orbits, but he still didn't know **why.**

---

## Newton's wild idea: it's all the same falling (1687)

Then came **Isaac Newton**, and the legend says it started with an apple falling from a tree. Newton asked a question nobody had dared: *what if the Moon is falling for the exact same reason the apple is?* What if there's only **one** rule, and it works everywhere — on Earth *and* in the sky?

To explain it, he imagined a cannon on a *very* tall mountain — this is the famous **"Newton's cannonball."** Fire it gently and the ball arcs down and lands. Fire it harder and it lands farther. Now fire it **so hard** that as it falls toward Earth, the round Earth **curves away beneath it just as fast.** The ball keeps falling… and keeps missing the ground… forever. That's an orbit.

> **The Moon is Newton's cannonball.** It's falling toward Earth every single second — but it's also racing sideways so fast that Earth keeps curving away under it. It falls and falls and never arrives. That's *exactly* the simulation you built.

---

## So why doesn't the Moon crash into Earth?

Here's the answer you can now give from the inside:

1. **Earth's gravity really is pulling the Moon down.** It is *not* floating free. (You computed that pull yourself — about 0.0027 m/s², weak but not zero.)
2. **The Moon is also moving sideways, fast.** Every second gravity bends its path toward Earth a little…
3. **…but in that same second the Moon coasts sideways just enough that the curved Earth falls away beneath it.** It's *constantly falling and constantly missing.*

It doesn't crash because it never stops moving sideways, and it doesn't fly away because gravity never stops pulling. The two are perfectly balanced into a loop. **Falling + sideways speed = orbit.** One rule, the same for an apple and the Moon. Newton united the heavens and the Earth with a single idea — and you rebuilt it, step by step, in code.
