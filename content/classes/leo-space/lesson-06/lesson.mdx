## Big Idea: The Hardest Part Is Stopping

Getting *to* the Moon is mostly coasting. The terrifying part is the last few hundred meters — coming **down** without smashing into the ground. In 1969, Neil Armstrong landed the real Lunar Module by hand with about 25 seconds of fuel to spare. Today, you'll land one with code.

The whole problem is a battle between two accelerations:

- **Gravity** pulls you *down*. On the Moon it's gentle — only $g = 1.62\ \text{m/s}^2$, about a sixth of Earth's. (You found that exact number back in Lesson 1!)
- **Your engine** pushes you *up* with some thrust acceleration you control.

Whichever wins decides what your speed does. We call the leftover the **net acceleration**:

$$
a = (\text{thrust}) - g
$$

- Engine **weaker** than gravity (thrust $< g$) → net is downward → you keep descending, but **slower than a free fall**. Your engine is softening the landing.
- Engine **equal** to gravity (thrust $= g$) → net is zero → you drift down at a steady speed, like a feather.
- Engine **stronger** than gravity (thrust $> g$) → net is upward → you slow to a stop and start *rising* again. Fire too hard and you'll lift back off!

For a controlled landing, you want your thrust **close to but not more than** gravity — a gentle, drifting descent that kisses the ground.

## One Step at a Time

This is the same Euler loop you've used all along, with the acceleration being that tug-of-war. Up is positive, so a downward velocity is **negative** (you're falling):

```python
v = v + (thrust - g) * dt    # net acceleration changes your speed
h = h + v * dt               # your speed changes your height
```

Start the lander up at some height `h` with some downward velocity `v` (a negative number), and repeat the two lines. When `h` reaches `0`, you've touched the ground — and the velocity at that instant is your **touchdown speed.**

```python
def land(h0, v0, thrust, g, dt, steps):
    h = h0
    v = v0
    for _ in range(steps):
        v = v + (thrust - g) * dt
        h = h + v * dt
        if h <= 0:        # touchdown!
            break
    return v              # the speed you hit at
```

> **Why the step limit?** If you set thrust *higher* than gravity, the lander slows, stops, and floats back up — it would never reach the ground, and the loop would run forever. The `steps` cap is a safety net. For a real landing, keep your thrust a touch below `g` so you actually come down.

---

## Did You Land — or Crash?

A spacecraft's legs can only take so much. Touch down too fast and you break something; touch down gently and you've made it. The cutoff we'll use is **2 m/s** — about walking-into-a-wall speed. So a landing is safe when the touchdown speed (ignoring its direction) is small enough:

```python
def is_safe(touchdown_speed):
    return abs(touchdown_speed) <= 2.0
```

The `abs()` throws away the minus sign, because we only care *how fast* you hit, not which way you were moving. A touchdown speed of $-1.5$ m/s is safe; $-3.5$ m/s is a crash.

The mission, {{FIRST_NAME}}: fire your engine hard enough that you drift down and `is_safe` comes back `True`. Too little thrust and you slam in. Too much and you never land. Just right, and you stick the landing — in pure Python.
