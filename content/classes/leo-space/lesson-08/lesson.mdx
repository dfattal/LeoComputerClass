But what if you'd *never* learned that formula? Could you still find the right speed — using nothing but your simulator? Yes. You'd try lots of speeds, measure how circular each orbit comes out, and **keep the best one.** That's an *optimizer*, and it's one of the most powerful ideas in all of computing.

Here's the thrilling part: when your search finishes, it'll land right on top of the formula. The machine will have **rediscovered the law of orbits** without being told it. That's your capstone.

## Step 1: Measure "How Circular?"

To search for the *roundest* orbit, you first need a way to *score* an orbit. Easy: simulate it, find its closest distance (perigee) and farthest distance (apogee), and subtract.

- A **perfect circle** has perigee = apogee, so the gap is **0**.
- A **stretched ellipse** has a big gap.

So a smaller gap means a rounder orbit. We'll call this the **roundness** score (really, the *un*-roundness — smaller is better):

```python
def roundness(M, r0, v, dt, steps):
    min_r, max_r = sim_orbit(M, r0, v, dt, steps)
    return max_r - min_r
```

You're given `sim_orbit` (it's your Lesson 2 loop, upgraded to track the smallest and biggest distance). Launch too slow and the orbit dips in on one side — big gap. Too fast and it bulges out — big gap. *Just right* and it's a clean circle — tiny gap.

---

## Step 2: Sweep and Keep the Best

Now the search. Instead of testing wild random speeds, we'll test speeds *near* the formula's answer by scaling it with a **factor** `f`:

- `f = 1.0` means "exactly the formula speed."
- `f = 0.9` means "10% slower," `f = 1.1` means "10% faster," and so on.

We sweep `f` from 0.80 up to 1.20, score each one's roundness, and remember the factor that scored lowest — exactly like the dart-mass sweep in your Motion Lab capstone:

```python
def best_factor(M, r0, dt, steps):
    vc = v_circle(M, r0)        # the formula's speed (given)
    best_f = 0
    best_score = 1e99           # start impossibly bad
    f = 0.80
    while f <= 1.20 + 1e-9:
        score = roundness(M, r0, f * vc, dt, steps)
        if score < best_score:  # rounder than anything so far?
            best_score = score
            best_f = f
        f = f + 0.02
    return round(best_f, 2)
```

If your simulator and your physics are right, the winning factor will come out to **1.00** — meaning the roundest orbit happens at *exactly* the formula speed. The search confirms the law.

---

## Step 3: The Payoff

Finally, turn that winning factor back into an actual speed:

```python
def best_speed(M, r0, dt, steps):
    return best_factor(M, r0, dt, steps) * v_circle(M, r0)
```

Run it for Earth at $r = 7{,}000$ km and your *searched* answer comes back as about **7,545.8 m/s** — the very same number the Lesson 2 formula gives. Two completely different roads — one a pencil-and-paper formula, one a brute-force computer search — arriving at the *identical* answer.

That agreement is the deepest idea in this whole class. It's how scientists *trust* a result: when the theory and the simulation shake hands, you know you've found something real.
