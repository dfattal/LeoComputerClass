## Big Idea: Real Orbits Are Ovals

Last lesson you found the *one* speed that makes a perfect circle. But what if you go a little faster? You don't fly away — you swing out into a **stretched oval** and then come right back. That oval is called an **ellipse**, and it's the shape of almost every real orbit: planets, comets, satellites, the Moon.

Two special points on every elliptical orbit:

- **Perigee** — the *closest* you get to the planet. You're moving *fastest* here (gravity has been pulling you in, speeding you up).
- **Apogee** — the *farthest* you get. You're moving *slowest* here (you've been climbing against gravity, trading speed for height).

It's exactly like a skateboarder in a halfpipe: fastest at the bottom, slowest at the top, trading speed for height and back again — forever. {{FIRST_NAME}}, your satellite does the same thing, swooping fast past the planet and coasting slowly way out at the top.

---

## How Stretched? Eccentricity

To describe the *shape* of an orbit with a single number, we use **eccentricity**. It compares how different the apogee and perigee are:

$$
e = \frac{r_{apo} - r_{peri}}{r_{apo} + r_{peri}}
$$

- $e = 0$ → a **perfect circle** (apogee equals perigee, top and bottom are nowhere).
- $e = 0.3$ → a **mildly squished** oval.
- $e$ close to $1$ → a **long, dramatic** ellipse (like a comet that dives past the Sun and shoots far away).

Earth's orbit around the Sun has $e \approx 0.017$ — almost a perfect circle. Halley's Comet has $e \approx 0.97$ — a wildly stretched cigar.

---

## The Size of an Orbit: the Semi-Major Axis

An ellipse's "size" is set by its **semi-major axis**, written $a$ — basically the average of its closest and farthest distances:

$$
a = \frac{r_{peri} + r_{apo}}{2}
$$

For a circle, $r_{peri} = r_{apo}$, so $a$ is just the radius — nice and consistent. This single number $a$ turns out to control something amazing: *how long the orbit takes.*

---

## Kepler's Third Law: Size Tells You Time

Four hundred years ago, Johannes Kepler stared at planet data and found a stunning pattern: an orbit's **period** (the time for one full lap) depends *only* on its semi-major axis $a$ — not on its shape, not on its mass. Newton later showed why, and gave us the formula:

$$
T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{a^3}{G M}}
$$

In words: *cube the size, divide by the planet's pull, square-root it.* Bigger orbits take dramatically longer — double the size and the period grows by $2^{1.5} \approx 2.8$ times.

Check it on something you know — a low Earth orbit ($a = 7{,}000$ km):

$$
T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{(7\times10^6)^3}{G \cdot 5.972\times10^{24}}} \approx 5{,}829 \text{ seconds} \approx 97 \text{ minutes}
$$

That's why astronauts on the Space Station see a sunrise every 90-ish minutes — they really do loop the whole Earth that fast! And our own planet? Plug in Earth's orbit around the Sun and Kepler's law spits out **31.5 million seconds** — one year. The math knows.

## Finding the Apogee with Code

The formulas above describe an orbit you already know the shape of. But your **simulator** can *discover* the shape. Launch a satellite a little faster than circular speed and let the loop run: it'll swing outward, and somewhere out there it reaches its farthest point — the apogee — before falling back.

The trick in code is simple: as you step, keep a running record of the **biggest distance** you've seen so far.

```python
farthest = r0
# inside the loop, after updating position:
d = math.sqrt(x*x + y*y)
if d > farthest:
    farthest = d
```

Give the loop *enough steps* to reach the top of the arc (a stretched orbit takes longer — remember Kepler!), and `farthest` will hold the apogee distance. You've measured an orbit's shape with nothing but a stepping loop.
