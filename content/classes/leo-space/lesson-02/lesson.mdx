## Big Idea: Newton's Cannonball

Isaac Newton imagined a cannon on top of an impossibly tall mountain, firing a cannonball sideways.

- Fire it **gently** and it arcs through the air and lands a little way off — you already simulated this in Motion Lab.
- Fire it **harder** and it lands farther away, because while it falls, the round Earth is also curving away beneath it.
- Fire it **just right** and here's the magic: the ground curves away *exactly* as fast as the ball falls. The cannonball keeps falling forever and **never hits the ground.** It's in **orbit.**

That's the whole secret, {{FIRST_NAME}}: an orbit isn't "escaping gravity." Gravity is pulling the whole time. An orbit is **falling so fast sideways that you keep missing the planet.** The Moon is doing it right now. So is every satellite over your head.

---

## How Fast Is "Just Right"?

For a perfect circle, there's one exact speed. We can find it by matching two things you already know.

Going in a circle of radius $r$ requires a constant inward pull (the same reason a ball on a string tugs your hand). The acceleration needed to hold a circle is:

$$
a = \frac{v^2}{r}
$$

And the pull *available* is gravity, which you learned last lesson:

$$
a = \frac{G M}{r^2}
$$

For a circle to hold, the pull you need has to equal the pull you have. Set them equal:

$$
\frac{v^2}{r} = \frac{G M}{r^2}
$$

Multiply both sides by $r$ and take the square root, and out pops the **circular orbital speed**:

$$
v = \sqrt{\frac{G M}{r}}
$$

Plug in Earth and a low orbit ($r = 7{,}000$ km from the center) and you get about **7,546 m/s** — roughly **27,000 km/h!** That's why reaching orbit is so hard: it's not about going *up*, it's about going *sideways* unbelievably fast.

> **Farther out = slower.** Notice $r$ is on the bottom: the higher your orbit, the *slower* you go. The Moon, way out at 384,000 km, only needs about 1,000 m/s to hold its orbit. Gravity is weaker out there, so less speed is needed to balance it.

## Gravity Points *Somewhere* Now

In Motion Lab, gravity always pointed straight down. In space there is no "down" — there's only **toward the planet.** As your satellite swings around, the direction of the pull keeps changing. So we need gravity as a **2D vector**: two numbers, `ax` and `ay`.

Put the planet at the center, $(0, 0)$, and your satellite at position $(x, y)$. Its distance from the center is the Pythagorean theorem:

$$
r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}
$$

The *strength* of the pull is the same $G M / r^2$ as always. We just have to split it into an x-part and a y-part that point back toward the center. The trick is to scale by $x/r$ and $y/r$:

$$
a_x = -\,\frac{G M}{r^2}\cdot\frac{x}{r} = -\,\frac{G M\, x}{r^3}
\qquad
a_y = -\,\frac{G M\, y}{r^3}
$$

The minus signs mean "point back toward the center." If you're to the *right* of the planet (positive $x$), gravity pulls you *left* (negative $a_x$). Makes sense!

> **Why divide by $r^3$?** One factor of $r^2$ is the usual gravity weakening. The extra $/r$ (hidden in the $x/r$) is just turning the direction into a clean "unit" arrow of length 1. Don't overthink it — the formula handles the aiming for you.

---

## The 2D Euler Loop

Your stepping loop barely changes — you just do everything **twice**, once for x and once for y:

```python
r = math.sqrt(x*x + y*y)      # how far from the planet
ax = -G * M * x / r**3        # gravity's x-part (points toward center)
ay = -G * M * y / r**3        # gravity's y-part
vx = vx + ax * dt             # update both velocity parts
vy = vy + ay * dt
x = x + vx * dt               # then update both position parts
y = y + vy * dt
```

Same rhythm as always: **update velocity first, then position.** Repeat it a few thousand times and you've got a satellite tracing a circle around the Earth — pure code, no kit, no magic.

To launch into a circle, you start off to one side, say at $(r, 0)$, and fire **sideways** (straight up the screen) at exactly the orbital speed: velocity $(0, v)$. Gravity does the rest.

## Goldilocks: Too Slow, Too Fast, Just Right

Once you can simulate it, you can *play*:

- **Just right** ($v = \sqrt{GM/r}$): a perfect circle. Your distance from the planet stays the same the whole way around.
- **Too slow**: gravity wins. You spiral inward and your distance shrinks — you're crashing.
- **Too fast**: you climb away from the planet, stretching the circle into a long oval (that's next lesson — ellipses!).

You'll test all three in the exercises by checking the satellite's distance from the center after the simulation runs.
