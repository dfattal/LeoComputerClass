## Big Idea

Last lesson you cracked a Caesar cipher by trying all 26 keys. The problem with Caesar is that every letter shifts by the *same* amount — so once you know where one letter goes, you know where they all go.

The fix: let **every letter map to whatever you want.** Scramble the whole alphabet into a secret order, and use that as your key:

```text
plain:  a b c d e f g h i j k l m n o p q r s t u v w x y z
key:    q w e r t y u i o p a s d f g h j k l z x c v b n m
```

Now `a` becomes `q`, `b` becomes `w`, `c` becomes `e`, and so on. There's no single "shift" — each letter has its own secret partner. This is a **substitution cipher**: every letter is *substituted* for another, following your scrambled key.

To encrypt `hello`:
- `h` → `i`, `e` → `t`, `l` → `s`, `l` → `s`, `o` → `g`  ⟶ `itssg`

> **It's just a lookup table.** Your key is really a dictionary: "when you see `a`, write `q`." In code we can store that scramble as a 26-letter string, where the 1st letter is what `a` becomes, the 2nd is what `b` becomes, and so on. Position in the alphabet tells you which key letter to use.

---

## Why This Cipher Laughs at Brute Force

How many secret keys are possible now? You're arranging all 26 letters into a new order. The first letter can be any of 26, the next any of the remaining 25, then 24… That's

$$
26! = 26 \times 25 \times 24 \times \cdots \times 1 \approx 4 \times 10^{26}
$$

That's **403,291,461,126,605,635,584,000,000** keys. If a computer tried a billion keys every second, going through them all would take **far longer than the age of the universe.** Brute force is dead. 🪦

> **26! is enormous.** The Caesar cipher had 26 keys — a bike lock. The substitution cipher has more keys than there are grains of sand on Earth, by a huge margin. You cannot try them all. (Next lesson, though, you'll learn the *clever* way to crack it anyway — counting beats guessing.)

## Encrypting With a Key

Encrypting is a lookup. For each letter in the message, find its position in the normal alphabet (`a`=0, `b`=1, …), and use that position to pick the key letter:

```python
key[ord(c) - 97]    # the cipher letter for plain letter c
```

As always: letters `a`–`z` get substituted; spaces and other characters pass through unchanged.

---

## The Reverse Key (for Decrypting)

To decrypt, you need to go **backwards**: if `a` → `q`, then seeing a `q` means it was an `a`. So you need the **reverse key** — the decoding alphabet.

Building it is a neat puzzle. Walk through your key. The key says position `0` (`a`) maps to `q`. So in the reverse key, the slot for `q` should hold `a`. Do that for all 26 letters and you've inverted the mapping:

```text
key:         q w e r t y u i o p a s d f g h j k l z x c v b n m
reverse key: k x v m c n o p h q r s z y i j a d l e g w b u f t
```

Once you have the reverse key, **decrypting is just encrypting with the reverse key.** Same lookup, different table. And of course: encrypt then decrypt must return the original — the round-trip is your proof.

---

## What You'll Build

1. `substitute(message, key)` — encrypt: replace each letter using the 26-letter `key`.
2. `invert_key(key)` — build and return the reverse key (the decoding alphabet).
3. `unsubstitute(message, key)` — decrypt: substitute using the reverse key.
