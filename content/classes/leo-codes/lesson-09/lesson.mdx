## Round 1: Ancient generals hide their orders

The oldest secret codes we know of are over 2,000 years old. A Roman general named **Julius Caesar** really did shift his letters down the alphabet to keep his battle plans secret — that's why your very first cipher is called the **Caesar cipher.** For a long time, simple tricks like this *worked*, because most people couldn't even read, let alone break a code.

But there was a weakness, and you found it yourself: there are only 26 shifts, so you can just **try them all.** Code-makers needed something stronger.

---

## Round 2: Scramble everything — and the codebreakers strike back

So they scrambled the *whole* alphabet (your substitution cipher) — *billions* of possible keys, way too many to try one by one. It seemed unbreakable.

Then, over 1,000 years ago, a brilliant scholar named **Al-Kindi**, in Baghdad, invented the trick that broke it: **frequency analysis.** He noticed that in any language some letters show up way more than others ('e' is everywhere in English). Count the symbols, and the most common one is probably 'e' — and the code unravels. You did exactly this. With one clever idea, the "unbreakable" code fell.

This is the heartbeat of the whole story: **make a code → someone finds its weakness → make a better code.** Over and over, for centuries.

---

## Round 3: Machines join the fight (World War II)

Fast-forward to the 1940s. Germany built a typewriter-like machine called **Enigma** that scrambled messages in a way so complex that trying all the keys would take *millions of years.* It looked truly unbreakable.

So the codebreakers built **machines of their own.** At a secret base in England called Bletchley Park, a team led by **Alan Turing** — the same genius who imagined the modern computer — built electro-mechanical machines to crack Enigma every single day. Breaking that code helped end the war years sooner and saved countless lives. The fight between codes and codebreakers literally **gave birth to the computer.**

---

## Round 4: The impossible problem — and the idea that runs the internet

By now every good code had the same catch, and maybe you felt it too: to send a secret, **both people need the same key first.** But how do you agree on a key with someone **far away**, when a spy might be listening to *everything* you say? For thousands of years, everyone assumed you simply *couldn't.* You had to meet in secret first.

Then in the 1970s, a few mathematicians — **Whitfield Diffie, Martin Hellman, Ralph Merkle**, and then **Rivest, Shamir, and Adleman (RSA)** — discovered something that sounds impossible: a way to agree on a secret **out in the open**, with the spy hearing every word, and *still* end up with a key only the two of you know.

You built both halves of this magic:

- **Color-mixing key exchange** — paint that's easy to mix but impossible to un-mix, so two people end up with the same secret color even though everyone watched.
- **RSA** — a "public lock" anyone can click shut, that only your **private key** can open.

> **This is the idea that protects you every day.** Every time you see a little padlock in a web address, your computer and that website are doing exactly what you built — agreeing on a secret key in the open, where anyone could listen, and still keeping it safe.
