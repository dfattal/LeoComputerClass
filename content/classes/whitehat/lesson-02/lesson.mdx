## Brute force: try everything

When there's no clever trick, a hacker just guesses — but *systematically*. Start at `0000`, then `0001`, then `0002`... all the way up until the lock clicks. That's **brute force**.

It sounds slow, but you're not typing by hand — your computer is. A computer can try **thousands of guesses every second**. A 4-digit PIN only has 10,000 possible codes, so a computer chews through all of them in the blink of an eye.

```python
crack_pin("0042")   # tries 0000, 0001, ... and stops at -> "0042"
```

Your cracker tries codes in order and returns the one that matches. The trick is to make each guess the **same length** as the secret, with leading zeros: guess number 42 should be `"0042"`, not `"42"`. Python's `zfill` does exactly that:

```python
str(42).zfill(4)    # -> "0042"
```

## Why longer is stronger 📏

Here's the secret every password expert knows. Each extra digit doesn't *add* to the work — it **multiplies** it by 10:

| Digits | Possible codes |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | 10 |
| 2 | 100 |
| 4 | 10,000 |
| 6 | 1,000,000 |
| 8 | 100,000,000 |

A 4-digit PIN: cracked instantly. An 8-digit one: now your computer has a *hundred million* codes to grind through. That jump is why "make your password longer" is the best advice in security. It's not about being clever — it's about giving the attacker too many doors to try.

```python
combos(4)   # -> 10000
combos(8)   # -> 100000000
```
