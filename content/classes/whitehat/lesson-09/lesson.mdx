## A short history of the cat-and-mouse game

Security has always been two sides chasing each other, each one forcing the other to get smarter.

- **The 1960s — the first hackers.** At MIT, "hacker" simply meant someone who understood the new computers so deeply they could make them do clever, unexpected things. It was a *compliment*. Curiosity, not crime.
- **1988 — the worm that woke everyone up.** A student wrote a program that used a **buffer overflow** — the exact attack you pulled off in Lesson 4 — to copy itself across the whole early internet. It was meant as an experiment, but it spread out of control and froze thousands of machines. Suddenly the world realized: software has *holes*, and holes have *consequences*.
- **The 1990s–2000s — the web explodes.** As shopping and banking moved online, attackers found that the simplest tricks worked best: **guessing passwords**, **SQL injection**, **sniffing** unencrypted traffic. Every attack you learned this term comes from this era — and so does every defense.
- **Today — the defenders fight back.** Now we **salt** every password, **encrypt** every connection (that's the 🔒 in your browser), **check** every buffer, and **clean** every input. And companies *hire* white hats — called penetration testers — to attack their own systems on purpose, so the holes get found by friends instead of crooks.

## Why it never ends

Here's the thing: every time defenders close a hole, attackers look for a new one. Every time attackers find a trick, defenders invent a patch. It's a **forever game of cat and mouse** — and that's not a failure, it's just how security works. The goal isn't to "win" once; it's to keep making the attacker's job harder than it's worth.

And the single thing that makes *you* one of the good guys isn't the skills — it's the one rule you started with: **permission.** The exact same buffer overflow is a crime in one place and a paycheck in another. The code is identical. The *consent* is everything.
