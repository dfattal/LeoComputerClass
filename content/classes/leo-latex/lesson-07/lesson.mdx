## Big Idea

Everything in your book has been heading here. You have the **tower** ($e^x$, Lesson 4) and you have the **quarter-turn number** ($i$, Lesson 6). Chapter 8 of your curriculum asked the irresistible question: *what happens if you feed a rotation into the tower?*

Plug $ix$ into the series and the powers of $i$ start cycling — $i, -1, -i, 1, i, -1, \dots$ — sorting the tower's floors into two alternating families: the floors that stay **real**, and the floors that keep an **$i$**. You named those two families. They are cosine and sine:

$$e^{ix} = \cos(x) + i\sin(x)$$

No triangles. No circles. You *built* sine and cosine out of pure algebra. Today: publish the split — and then use last week's conjugate twins to summon geometry itself.

## Multi-Line Derivations: `align*`

Real proofs don't fit on one line. Open any math book: derivations stack, with every step's $=$ sign **lined up in a column**. The environment that does this is `align*`:

```
\begin{align*}
(\text{big thing}) &= \text{first step} \\
&= \text{final answer}
\end{align*}
```

Three new marks, each with one job:

- `\begin{align*}` and `\end{align*}` — open and close the derivation block
- `&=` — the ampersand pins this spot to the alignment column; every line's `&=` stacks
- `\\` — end of line, drop to the next

(The `*` means "no equation numbers" — book style for now.) Continuation lines start right at `&=`, with nothing before it — the math is *continuing*, not restarting.

And here's the good news about your grader: it reads an `align*` block as **one long chain of equals** — every line is another link, and every link gets verified numerically. The proof's *whole spine* is checked.

## Function Powers: `\cos^2`

"Cosine squared" attaches the power to the function name:

```
\cos^2(x) + \sin^2(x)
```

$$\cos^2(x) + \sin^2(x)$$

Same `^` you've always used, riding on `\cos` directly. (Writing $\cos(x)^2$ is legal but ambiguous to readers; $\cos^2(x)$ is book style.)

---

## Summoning the Circle

Now the chapter's magic trick, in three moves you *already own*:

1. Take the split and its **conjugate twin** (Lesson 6): $\cos(x) + i\sin(x)$ and $\cos(x) - i\sin(x)$.
2. Multiply the twins — and the $i$'s annihilate, exactly like $(2+3i)(2-3i)$ did: out comes $\cos^2(x) + \sin^2(x)$.
3. But the twins are also $e^{ix}$ and $e^{-ix}$, and your Chapter 4 exponent rule says their product is $e^0 = 1$.

So:

$$\cos^2(x) + \sin^2(x) = 1$$

That equation **is the unit circle** — every point $(\cos x, \sin x)$ sits at distance 1. You summoned the circle, and all of triangle-trigonometry behind it, from a series and an imaginary number. This is the most magician-like page of your book; give it the two-line `align*` it deserves.

## One More Gift

Since sine and cosine are now just infinite polynomials, their derivatives come from the **power rule** — Lesson 1's machine, running floor by floor down the tower:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\sin(x) = \cos(x)$$

The grader will verify it the honest way: nudge $x$, measure sine's slope, compare to your cosine. Page seven awaits.
