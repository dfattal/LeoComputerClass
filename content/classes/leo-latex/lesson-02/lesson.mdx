## Big Idea

On paper, you derived two of the most powerful rules in calculus. The **product rule** came from a growing rectangle: if the width $f$ and the height $g$ are both growing, the new area comes from *both* edges —

$$(fg)' = f'g + fg'$$

And the **chain rule** came from interlocked gears: if gear A turns 3× faster than gear B, and B turns 4× faster than C, then A turns $3 \times 4 = 12$× faster than C. Rates of nested machines **multiply**:

$$\left( f(g(x)) \right)' = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$$

Today you typeset these rules *doing real work* — applied to actual functions. And here's the fun part: when you press **Check**, the grader doesn't take your word for it. It **takes the derivative itself**, numerically, and compares. You're being checked by a machine that does calculus.

---

## The Derivative Operator Is Just a Fraction

Look closely at how mathematicians write "the derivative of":

```
\frac{d}{dx}
```

$$\frac{d}{dx}$$

That's the same `\frac` you learned last week — with a $d$ on top and $dx$ on the bottom. (That's no accident: a derivative *is* a ratio of tiny changes, the very limit you typeset in Lesson 1.) Put the thing you're differentiating right after it:

```
\frac{d}{dx} x^3
```

$$\frac{d}{dx} x^3$$

---

## The Multiplication Dot: `\cdot`

When you write the chain rule's "outer times inner", you need a multiplication sign. A `*` is for code; an `x` is a letter. The professional mark is a centered dot:

```
3(x^2+1)^2 \cdot 2x
```

$$3(x^2+1)^2 \cdot 2x$$

Use `\cdot` whenever two *separate* results get multiplied. (Side-by-side like $2x\sin(x)$ doesn't need one — touching means multiplied.)

---

## Growing Brackets: `\left(` and `\right)`

Wrap the function you're differentiating in brackets so the reader can see exactly what the operator grabs. And when what's inside is *tall* — a fraction, a power tower — plain `( )` look like they're being crushed. The fix you met last week works on every bracket shape:

```
\frac{d}{dx}\left( x^2 \sin(x) \right)
```

$$\frac{d}{dx}\left( x^2 \sin(x) \right)$$

`\left(` and `\right)` always travel as a pair. They measure what's between them and stretch to fit. Editors *will* notice if your brackets don't fit the math inside them.

---

## How the Grader Checks a Derivative

When you write

$$\frac{d}{dx}\left( x^2 \sin(x) \right) = 2x\sin(x) + x^2\cos(x)$$

the grader picks sneaky values of $x$, computes the left side **as an actual derivative** (it nudges $x$ a tiny bit and measures the change — the same limit idea from Lesson 1!), computes your right side as a formula, and demands they match. Every value. If you forget a term — say you write $2x\sin(x)$ and drop the $x^2\cos(x)$ — the numbers disagree instantly and it tells you where.

There is no way to bluff a derivative past this thing. Which means: when your page goes green, **your calculus is certified correct.**
