# Lesson 6 — Exercises: A New Dimension

## How This Works

Same flow — but this week the grader is doing **complex arithmetic**. Every chain is verified in both coordinates: real part *and* imaginary part.

---

## Exercise 1: The Quarter-Turn Number

Under `%% the-rotation`, typeset the definition as a working chain:

$$i^2 = i \cdot i = \;?$$

**You'll need:** `i^2`, `\cdot`, and a minus sign.

**Hints:**
- Three links: the power, the spelled-out product (with `\cdot`), the landing point.
- Two quarter-turns = one half-turn = facing backwards on the line.
- The grader multiplies $i \cdot i$ as a genuine complex product. There is no faking it.

---

## Exercise 2: Conjugate Partners

Under `%% conjugate-partners`, multiply the mirror twins and show the middle step:

$$(2+3i)(2-3i) = \;? - \;?i^2 = \;?$$

**You'll need:** parentheses side by side (touching means multiplied), `i^2` in the middle step.

**Hints:**
- It's a difference of squares: $first^2 - (3i)^2 = 4 - 9i^2$.
- Then the definition flips the sign: $-9i^2 = +9$. Four plus nine.
- The final answer is **purely real** — the twins always cancel the $i$'s. The grader confirms the imaginary part is exactly zero.

---

## Exercise 3: Multiply to Rotate

Under `%% quarter-turn`, rotate the point $3+2i$ by multiplying it by $i$ — show the distribution:

$$i\left(3+2i\right) = \;? + \;? = \;?$$

**You'll need:** `\left( \right)` around the point, then distribute.

**Hints:**
- $i$ times $3$ is $3i$; $i$ times $2i$ is $2i^2$, and you know what $i^2$ does.
- The final answer keeps its $i$: $-2 + 3i$. **Don't simplify it away** — this answer is a point, not a length.
- Check the picture in your head: $(3,2) \to (-2,3)$. Same arrow, swung 90°. The grader verifies both coordinates separately and names the one that's off.
