## Big Idea

Look back at your pages. A limit became a derivative. Rules for compound machines. Factorials, the choose symbol, Pascal's secret. An infinite tower that replicates itself. A mirror that turns multiplication into addition. A quarter-turn number that opened a second dimension. A split that summoned the circle out of algebra.

Every one of those pages was a load-bearing wall. Today you set the keystone.

In the great split, let $x$ be $\pi$ — half a turn around the circle. Cosine of $\pi$ is $-1$; sine of $\pi$ is $0$. The imaginary family vanishes entirely, and what remains is the most famous equation in mathematics:

$$e^{i\pi} = -1$$

Five symbols — $e$ from growth, $i$ from rotation, $\pi$ from the circle, $1$ and $-$ from plain counting — none of which seem to belong together, locking into one tiny statement. People get this tattooed. **You derived it.** Every link in its chain is a chapter of your own book.

---

## The Grader Meets the Identity

Typeset the chain

$$e^{i\pi} = \cos(\pi) + i\sin(\pi) = -1$$

and think about what Check actually does: it computes $e^{i\pi}$ — genuinely, the complex exponential, the same tower of Lesson 4 fed with $i\pi$ — and watches it land on $-1 + 0i$, exactly. Then it checks your middle step too. A machine confirming Euler's identity, link by link, on your page. (The same `\pi` command from your bounds works anywhere — and `e^{i\pi}` needs *all three* characters inside the exponent braces.)

## The Impossible Logarithm

Now the move from your Chapter 10 — the one that feels like it should be against the rules. Take $\ln$ of both sides:

$$\ln(-1) = i\pi$$

Ask any calculator for $\ln(-1)$ and it says **ERROR** — on the number *line*, nothing's exponential ever lands at $-1$. But your number line burst two lessons ago. In the plane there's a perfectly good path to $-1$: go half a turn. The logarithm of a negative number isn't impossible — it's just **not on the line**. It's $i\pi$ steps in the new direction.

Your grader lives in the plane, so it verifies this one natively: the real part of $\ln(-1)$ is $0$, the imaginary part is $\pi = 3.14159\dots$ Both coordinates, checked. Write the line no calculator will say.

---

## Closing the Loop

A book's last page should answer its first. Page one of your book computed an area with the Fundamental Theorem. So close with one more — now wearing everything you've learned:

$$\int_0^{\pi} \sin(x)\,dx = \left[ -\cos(x) \right]_0^{\pi} = 2$$

Read what's in that one line: the integral from page one. The bounds carrying $\pi$ — *your* $\pi$, the cosine-root from Chapter 10. The antiderivative of sine — which you can only write because of the great split. The evaluation bracket — the FTC itself. And the area under one full arch of the sine wave is… exactly $2$. Not $2.0000017$. **Two.** The universe being tidy.

(Watch the small craft: the minus sign *inside* the bracket, the bounds hanging off `\right]`, the `\,` before $dx$ — page-one habits, summit-level math.)
