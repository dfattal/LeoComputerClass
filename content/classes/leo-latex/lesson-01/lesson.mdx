## Big Idea

You've already done something most adults never do: you **derived** calculus from first principles. The limit definition of the derivative. The power rule. Integrals as area. That math lives in your head and on scratch paper.

But look at any real math book — the formulas are *beautiful*. Crisp fractions, stretching brackets, perfect little exponents. Nobody draws those by hand. Every mathematician and physicist on Earth writes them in a language called **LaTeX** (say it "LAH-tek").

LaTeX is like Python, but instead of *computing* things, it *typesets* them. You write a recipe in plain text, and out comes a page that looks like it was printed in a textbook. In this class you're going to typeset **your own math book** — chapter by chapter, all the way to the most famous equation in mathematics, $e^{i\pi} = -1$. This week: page one.

---

## The Two Magic Dollars

LaTeX has two modes, and you switch with dollar signs:

- `$ … $` — **inline math**, for math inside a sentence. `$f(x) = x^2$` gives you $f(x) = x^2$ right in the middle of your text.
- `$$ … $$` — **display math**, for math that deserves its own line, centered like in a book.

Everything else you type is just normal text. One more trick: any line starting with `%` is a **comment** — notes to yourself the reader never sees. (And `%%` lines are special markers that tell the checker where each exercise lives. Don't delete those!)

---

## Powers and Subscripts

Two of the most-used symbols in all of math:

- `^` raises: `x^2` → $x^2$
- `_` lowers: `x_0` → $x_0$

**The brace rule** — this one matters: `^` and `_` only grab **one character**. If your exponent is longer, wrap it in braces `{ }`:

- `x^23` → $x^23$ ← LaTeX read that as "x squared, then a 3"!
- `x^{23}` → $x^{23}$ ← braces say "all of this goes up"
- `x^{3/2}` → $x^{3/2}$ — you'll need exactly this one today

---

## Fractions: `\frac`

Commands in LaTeX start with a backslash `\`. The king of them all:

```
\frac{top}{bottom}
```

`\frac{2}{3}` → $\frac{2}{3}$. And fractions nest — `\frac{x+1}{\frac{1}{2}}` works fine. Never write `2/3` in display math in this class. We're typesetting like professionals now.

---

## Roots: `\sqrt`

`\sqrt{x}` → $\sqrt{x}$. The braces hold whatever lives under the roof: `\sqrt{x+1}` → $\sqrt{x+1}$. (Want a cube root? `\sqrt[3]{27}` → $\sqrt[3]{27}$.)

---

## The Limit — Math You Already Own

Remember the machine you built that finds the slope at a single frozen instant? Here is how the pros write it:

```
\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}
```

$$\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$$

Read the pieces:
- `\lim` — the limit command
- `_{h \to 0}` — a *subscript* holding "$h$ approaches $0$"; `\to` draws the arrow $\to$
- then the difference quotient as one big `\frac`

You **derived** this. Now you can **publish** it.

---

## Integrals — and the Little `dx`

The integral sign is `\int`, and bounds attach exactly like subscripts and powers:

```
\int_0^1 \sqrt{x} \, dx
```

$$\int_0^1 \sqrt{x} \, dx$$

Two professional touches:
- `_0^1` — lower bound down, upper bound up. Same `_` and `^` you already know!
- `\,` — a **thin space** before the $dx$. Tiny detail, but it's what separates book-quality typesetting from homework. Editors notice.

---

## The Evaluation Bracket — FTC in One Symbol

When you compute an integral, there's that middle step where you've found the antiderivative and you're about to plug in the bounds. Mathematicians write it with a tall bracket:

$$\int_0^1 \sqrt{x}\,dx = \left[ \frac{2}{3} x^{3/2} \right]_0^1 = \frac{2}{3}$$

That bracket **is** the Fundamental Theorem you derived: the antiderivative at the top bound, minus it at the bottom. The code:

```
\left[ \frac{2}{3} x^{3/2} \right]_0^1
```

`\left[` and `\right]` are a team — they **grow the brackets** to fit whatever's inside. (Plain `[ ]` would look tiny and sad next to a tall fraction. Try both and see!) The `_0^1` after `\right]` hangs the bounds on the bracket.

## The Grader Checks Your MATH

Here's the part that makes this class different from every LaTeX tutorial on Earth. When you press **Check my page**, three graders read your work, in order:

1. **Does it compile?** — like a Python syntax error, but for LaTeX.
2. **Did you use the right tools?** — a real `\frac`, a real `\lim`, no `2/3` shortcuts.
3. **Is the math TRUE?** — it takes every `=` in your chain and *tests it with actual numbers*. Write $\int_0^1 \sqrt{x}\,dx = \frac{3}{2}$ and it will catch you, because it *computes the integral* and gets $\frac{2}{3}$.

And for an integral with no bounds — like $\int \frac{dx}{x+1}$ — the grader does something you'll recognize: it **takes the derivative of your answer** and checks it lands back on the thing inside the integral. That's the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, working as your proofreader.

So you can't bluff this class with pretty symbols. The page has to *compile*, *look* professional, and *be true*. Just like a real publication.
