## update() is the conductor

An orchestra has a violin, a drum, a trumpet — but somebody has to wave the baton
and tell them *when* to play. That person is the **conductor**.

`update(state, input)` is your conductor. It doesn't make any sound by itself. Every
frame — 60 times a second — it points at each instrument in turn and says "now you":

```javascript
// one frame, in order:
let ball   = bounce(step(state.ball), W, H);       // move + wall-bounce
const paddle = movePaddle(state.paddle, input, W);  // steer the paddle
// ...then check the paddle, the bricks, the floor...
return next;                                        // hand back the new world
```

That's the whole secret of a game: one function, called over and over, that takes
the world as it is now and returns the world one tick later.

## Keep it pure

Same rule as always — and it's the most important one. `update` builds a **brand-new**
state and returns it. It never reaches into the `state` it was handed and changes it.

Think of each frame as a fresh photo, not a scribble on the old one:

```javascript
let next = { ball: ball, paddle: paddle, bricks: state.bricks,
             score: state.score, lives: state.lives };
// ...adjust next, never the original state...
return next;
```

Pure functions are why we could test every helper one at a time — and it's why your
finished game won't glitch out.

## Publish it!

When `update` works, the panel on the right stops being a demo and becomes **your
game**. A ball you can keep alive. Bricks you can smash. A score that climbs. Three
lives on the line.

This is yours now — so **put it online!** Once your game runs, press the **Share**
button up in the toolbar. The studio publishes your game to its own web link and
hands you the URL. Anyone you send it to — a friend, a grandparent, your whole
class — can open that link and play your game right in their browser. **No sign-in,
no install.** Just your game, on the internet, made by you.

> 🎮 **Ship it.** Press **Share**, copy the link, and text it to someone. You didn't
> just learn to code a game — you built a real one from scratch, frame by frame, and
> published it to the world. That's what game developers do. Welcome to the studio.
