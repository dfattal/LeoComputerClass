## Big Idea

In Lesson 3 you built a flight that's actually realistic — drag and all. But a simulation you can't *measure* and *search* is just a pretty arc. This lesson adds the two skills that turn your simulator into an **engineering tool**:

1. **Measuring** — reading real numbers off the flight: How far? How high? How long in the air?
2. **Searching** — trying lots of designs automatically and keeping the best one.

That second skill is the big one. Instead of *guessing* the best launch angle, you'll have the computer **try every angle and tell you the winner**. That's exactly what real engineers do — and it's a tiny version of the design optimizer you'll build in the capstone.

## Measuring a Shot

Every measurement you want is hiding in the same loop you already wrote. You just watch for it as the dart flies:

- **Range** — the horizontal distance `x` at the moment it lands.
- **Peak height** — the largest `y` the dart ever reaches. Track it with "is this the highest so far?"
- **Hang time** — how long it stays airborne. Add `dt` to a running total every step.

```python
x = 0.0
y = 0.0
t = 0.0          # running clock
peak = 0.0       # highest point so far

while True:
    # ... drag physics, update vx, vy, x, y ...
    t = t + dt                  # the clock ticks
    if y > peak:
        peak = y                # new record height!
    if y <= 0:
        break

# range = x, peak height = peak, hang time = t
```

### Returning more than one number

A Python function can hand back **several values at once** — just put them in a list:

```python
return [x, peak, t]
```

Now one call to your simulator gives you the whole story of the shot. (You could also return a *tuple* `(x, peak, t)` — both work.)

## The Parameter Sweep: Let the Computer Choose

Here's the heart of the lesson. You want the launch angle that flies **farthest**. With drag there's no formula for it (remember Lesson 3!). So instead of solving, we **search**: try a whole range of angles, simulate each one, and keep track of the best.

In math we'd write this as "the angle that maximizes the range":

$$
\theta^\star = \arg\max_{\theta}\; R(\theta)
$$

In code, that fancy symbol is just a loop with a "best so far" tracker — the same pattern as finding the tallest kid in class:

```python
best_angle = None
best_range = -1            # nothing beats this yet

for angle in range(10, 71):        # try every angle from 10 to 70
    r = simulate_shot(speed, angle, k, m, dt)[0]   # how far did THIS angle fly?
    if r > best_range:             # a new winner?
        best_range = r
        best_angle = angle

# best_angle now holds the champion
```

Read it slowly: we start with no winner, try each angle, and every time we find one that flies farther than our current record, it becomes the new champion. After the loop, `best_angle` holds the answer. **You didn't solve an equation — you let the computer test 61 designs and report back.**

---

## The Payoff: Where's the Best Angle Now?

Run that sweep on our dart (`speed = 20`, `k = 0.01`, `m = 0.05`) and the winner comes back around **33°** — way below the textbook $45°$!

This is the cliffhanger from Lesson 2 fully resolved. In a vacuum, $45°$ wins. But a draggy dart spends *too long* fighting the air on a high, loopy $45°$ shot. A flatter shot in the low 30s gets to the target before the air can rob all its speed. **No formula told you that — your search did.**

> Try it yourself in the exercises: sweep the angles and watch the winner land in the low 30s. Then change the mass `m` and watch the best angle move. That's real design exploration.

## How Much Does Air Cost You?

Now that you can measure range, you can put a hard number on drag's damage. Compare the **vacuum** range (which *does* have a formula from Lesson 2) to the **drag** range from your simulator:

$$
R_\text{vac} = \frac{v_0^2 \sin(2\theta)}{g}
\qquad
\text{range lost} = R_\text{vac} - R_\text{drag}
$$

For that 20 m/s, 45° shot: the vacuum says **40.8 m**, your simulator says about **7.5 m** — so the air **steals over 33 meters**. That's not a rounding error; for a light dart, drag is the single biggest thing shaping the flight.

## Why This Matters for Your Blaster

You just leveled up from *simulating* a blaster to *interrogating* one. You can now ask design questions and let the computer answer them:

- "What angle should I aim?" → sweep angles, take the max.
- "How much range am I losing to air?" → compare to the vacuum formula.
- "What if my dart were heavier?" → change `m` and re-run the sweep.

That last one is the whole game in Phase 4: sweep over dart **mass**, spring **stiffness**, and barrel **length** to find the best blaster. Today's angle sweep is the exact same pattern, just over one knob. You've built the engine of the optimizer.
