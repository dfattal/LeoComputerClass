## The Famous Question: How Heavy Should the Dart Be?

Here's the puzzle that has no pencil-and-paper answer. Think about the dart's mass:

- A **light** dart leaves the barrel *fast* (remember $v = x_0\sqrt{k/m}$ — smaller $m$, bigger $v$)... but the air shoves it around mercilessly (drag deceleration is $\frac{k_\text{drag}}{m}\lvert\vec v\rvert v$ — smaller $m$, more slowing). It rockets out and then face-plants.
- A **heavy** dart leaves *slowly*... but it barrels through the air almost unbothered. It plods along but keeps going.

So somewhere between "too light" and "too heavy" is a **just-right** dart that flies the farthest. You saw it in Lesson 7's graph — now you'll *find* it:

```python
def best_mass(x0, k, angle_deg, kdrag, dt):
    best_m = 0.0
    best_range = -1
    m = 0.005
    while m <= 0.06 + 1e-9:           # try darts from 5 g to 60 g
        r = blast_range(m, x0, k, angle_deg, kdrag, dt)
        if r > best_range:            # a new champion?
            best_range = r
            best_m = m
        m = m + 0.005
    return best_m
```

That's the entire idea of engineering optimization in eleven lines: **try them all, keep the best.** No calculus shortcut — the simulator *is* the answer.

---

## The Optimum Moves

The best dart isn't a fixed number — it depends on the conditions. Crank up the air resistance (a fatter, draggier dart, or a windy day) and the *light* darts suffer even more, so the **optimal dart gets heavier**. Your optimizer discovers this automatically: run `best_mass` with more drag and watch the answer climb.

This is the real lesson of design: **there is no single "best blaster."** The best design depends on the dart, the air, the spring, the goal. The simulator is what lets you find the right answer for *your* situation — which is exactly why engineers build simulators.

## Tuning Two Knobs at Once

Why stop at one knob? You can search over the dart mass **and** the launch angle together — every combination — to find the design that flies absolutely farthest. It's just a loop inside a loop:

```python
def best_possible_range(x0, k, kdrag, dt):
    best_range = -1
    m = 0.005
    while m <= 0.06 + 1e-9:           # outer loop: dart mass
        angle = 15
        while angle <= 60 + 1e-9:     # inner loop: launch angle
            r = blast_range(m, x0, k, angle, kdrag, dt)
            if r > best_range:
                best_range = r
            angle = angle + 5         # step the angle
        m = m + 0.005                 # step the mass
    return best_range
```

The inner loop tries every angle for one dart mass; the outer loop walks through the masses — so together they try every mass-and-angle combination. Tuning *both* always does at least as well as tuning one — you've given the optimizer more freedom. Real designs have many knobs (spring stiffness, compression, barrel length, dart mass, angle); the search just grows more loops, but the idea never changes.

> **A word on cost:** every extra knob multiplies the number of designs to try. Two knobs with 12 and 10 settings is 120 simulations; add a third and it's thousands. This is why engineers think hard about *which* knobs matter most — and why fast simulators are worth so much.
