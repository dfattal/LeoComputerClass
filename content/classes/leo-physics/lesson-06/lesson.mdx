## Big Idea

Last lesson a spring pushed your dart. Now we swap it for a tank of **compressed air** — the other classic way to power a blaster. The physics rhymes with the spring (a push that fades as the dart moves), but the *reason* it fades is different and leads to a surprising design rule about barrel length.

Compressed air is just gas squished into a small space. Squished gas is at high **pressure**, and pressure pushing on the back of the dart is what launches it.

---

## Pressure Pushes on Area

Pressure is force spread over an area. To get the **force** on the dart, multiply the pressure by the area it pushes on — the cross-section of the barrel, $A$:

$$
F_\text{gas} = P \cdot A
$$

But the dart also has *outside* air pushing back on its front, at atmospheric pressure $P_\text{atm}$ (about $100{,}000$ Pa). So the **net** push is the difference:

$$
F_\text{net} = (P - P_\text{atm})\, A
$$

While the tank pressure $P$ is above atmospheric, the net force is forward and the dart accelerates. That's the launch.

---

## Boyle's Law: The Pressure Drops as the Gas Expands

Here's the twist that makes barrel length matter. As the dart moves forward, the gas behind it has **more room** — so it expands, and expanding gas **loses pressure**. The relationship (for gas that stays the same temperature) is **Boyle's law**:

$$
P \cdot V = P_0 \cdot V_0 \quad (\text{constant})
$$

Squeeze gas into half the volume and its pressure doubles; let it expand to double the volume and its pressure halves. If the tank starts with volume $V_0$ at pressure $P_0$, then after the dart has travelled a distance $d$ down a barrel of area $A$, the gas fills $V_0 + A\,d$, so:

$$
P = \frac{P_0\, V_0}{V_0 + A\, d}
$$

```python
def gas_pressure(P0, V0, A, d):
    return P0 * V0 / (V0 + A * d)
```

Just like the spring's force faded as it uncoiled, the gas's pressure fades as it expands — but now it can keep fading all the way *below* atmospheric.

---

## Down the Barrel

Same Euler loop, with the gas pressure recomputed every step:

```python
PATM = 100000.0
d = 0.0
v = 0.0
while d < barrel:
    P = gas_pressure(P0, V0, A, d)
    a = (P - PATM) * A / m      # net force / mass
    v = v + a * dt
    d = d + v * dt
# v is the muzzle velocity
```

The acceleration starts strong (high pressure), fades as the gas expands, and — here's the key — **can go negative**. Once the gas has expanded so much that $P < P_\text{atm}$, the outside air pushes harder than the tank, and the dart actually starts to **slow down**.

---

## The Sweet Spot: Why a Barrel Can Be Too Long

This is the headline of the whole lesson. Think about what happens as you make the barrel longer:

- **Too short:** the dart leaves while the gas is still pushing hard. You wasted pressure that could have done more work — like letting go of a swing too early.
- **Just right:** the dart leaves exactly when the pressure has dropped to atmospheric ($P = P_\text{atm}$). Every bit of the gas's extra push went into the dart.
- **Too long:** past that point the gas is below atmospheric, so the extra barrel **brakes** the dart instead of speeding it up. Longer is now *worse*.

So muzzle velocity rises, peaks, and falls as the barrel gets longer. There's a **best barrel length** — and it's exactly where the pressure reaches atmospheric:

$$
P = P_\text{atm} \;\Longrightarrow\; V_0 + A\, d^\star = \frac{P_0 V_0}{P_\text{atm}}
\;\Longrightarrow\;
d^\star = \frac{V_0}{A}\left(\frac{P_0}{P_\text{atm}} - 1\right)
$$

There's no need to memorize that — your simulator will *find* the peak for you by trying many barrel lengths, exactly like you found the best launch angle in Lesson 4.

---

## Connecting to Work and Energy

If you've met integrals, here's the elegant view: the speed the dart gains comes from the **work** the net force does along the barrel, which is the area under the force curve:

$$
\tfrac{1}{2} m v^2 = \int_0^{\text{barrel}} (P - P_\text{atm})\, A \; \mathrm{d}d
$$

The barrel length that maximizes that integral is exactly $d^\star$ — the point where the integrand $(P - P_\text{atm})A$ crosses zero. Stop collecting area while it's still positive and you've left work on the table; keep going past zero and you start *subtracting*. That's calculus telling you the same thing your simulator shows.

---

## Why This Matters for Your Blaster

You now have the second engine for your blaster, and a real engineering rule: **match the barrel length to the tank.** A bigger tank or higher pressure pushes useful pressure for longer, so it wants a *longer* barrel; a small tank wants a short one. There's no single "best barrel" — it depends on your design, and your simulator is how you find it.
