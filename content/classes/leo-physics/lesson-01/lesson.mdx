## Big Idea

You already know calculus. You know that if you have the position of a ball, its **velocity** is the derivative of position, and its **acceleration** is the derivative of velocity:

$$
v = \frac{dx}{dt} \qquad\qquad a = \frac{dv}{dt}
$$

In words: *velocity is how fast position is changing*, and *acceleration is how fast velocity is changing*. (Think of a car: position is where you are, velocity is the speedometer, acceleration is your foot pushing the gas or the brake.)

Here's the thing — in physics class you usually go **backwards**: you know the acceleration (gravity, a spring, air pushing back), and you want to find the position. That means **integrating**, and for the fun problems (a Nerf dart fighting air resistance, a spring whose push fades as it uncoils) the integral has **no clean formula**. You can't solve it with a pencil.

So we do what real scientists and engineers do: we let the **computer** do the calculus for us, one tiny step at a time. That trick is called the **Euler method**, and it's the engine that will power your whole blaster simulator.

---

## The Trick: Take Tiny Steps

A derivative like $a = \dfrac{dv}{dt}$ means "the change in velocity over a *tiny* change in time." The $d$ stands for an *infinitely* small amount.

A computer can't do *infinitely* small. But it can do **really small**. So we replace the tiny $d$ with a small but real time step we'll call $dt$ (say, one-hundredth of a second). The derivative $a = \dfrac{dv}{dt}$ turns into a plain multiplication — *over a small time $dt$, the velocity changes by $a$ times $dt$*:

$$
\Delta v = a \, dt
$$

So the **new** velocity is the old velocity plus that change:

```python
new_v = v + a * dt
```

The exact same logic works for position, because $v = \dfrac{dx}{dt}$:

$$
\Delta x = v \, dt
$$

```python
new_x = x + v * dt
```

That's the whole secret. Two lines. **Where you are nudges forward by your velocity; how fast you're going nudges forward by your acceleration.**

> **Flipbook analogy:** A movie isn't really moving — it's a stack of still pictures shown so fast it *looks* smooth. The Euler method draws motion the same way: one frozen frame every $dt$ seconds. Smaller $dt$ = more frames = smoother, more accurate motion.

---

## Putting It in a Loop

One step nudges the ball forward a tiny bit. To watch it move for a whole second, we just **repeat** the nudge many times. Here's a ball dropped from a height, falling under gravity:

```python
g = 9.8          # gravity pulls down at 9.8 meters per second, every second
dt = 0.01        # time step: one hundredth of a second
v = 0.0          # starts at rest (not moving yet)
h = 100.0        # starts 100 meters up

for _ in range(100):     # 100 steps of 0.01s = 1 second of falling
    v = v + (-g) * dt    # STEP 1: update velocity (gravity is negative — it points DOWN)
    h = h + v * dt       # STEP 2: update position using the new velocity
```

Walk through what happens:
- **Acceleration is $-g$** because we're calling *up* the positive direction, and gravity pulls *down*. Sign matters!
- Each loop, the velocity gets a little more negative (the ball speeds up downward), and the height drops a little more.
- After 100 steps, `h` holds the ball's height after 1 second.

> **Why update velocity first, then position?** We use the *freshly updated* velocity to move the ball. This little ordering choice (called *semi-implicit Euler*) keeps the simulation stable — and it's the same method we'll use later for springs, which bounce forever if you get it wrong. Stick with this order: **velocity, then position.**

---

## Is It Right? Check Against the Formula!

For a simple falling ball, you *do* know the exact answer from calculus. Integrating $a = -g$ twice gives the formula you've seen in physics:

$$
h(t) = h_0 - \tfrac{1}{2}\, g\, t^2
$$

```python
h = h0 - 0.5 * g * t**2
```

This is the golden rule of simulation: **whenever you can check your code against an exact answer, do it.** If your Euler loop and the formula agree on the easy problem, you can trust your loop on the *hard* problems where no formula exists.

Let's check. Drop a ball from 100 m for 1 second:

$$
h = 100 - \tfrac{1}{2}(9.8)(1)^2 = 100 - 4.9 = 95.1 \text{ m}
$$

- **Exact formula:** $95.1$ m
- **Euler loop (with $dt = 0.01$):** about $95.05$ m

So close! They don't match *perfectly*, and that's expected — Euler takes finite-sized steps, so it's always a tiny bit off. That gap is the **approximation error**.

**How do you shrink the error?** Use a smaller $dt$. With $dt = 0.001$ (ten times more steps), the loop gives about $95.095$ — even closer to $95.1$. Smaller steps, smoother flipbook, better answer. The trade-off: smaller $dt$ means more loops, so the computer does more work.

> Because your answers are approximations, the tests in this class accept anything **close enough** (within a small tolerance). You don't need to match to ten decimal places — you need to do the physics right.

## Why This Matters for Your Blaster

Right now we're dropping a boring ball. But this *exact* loop is the engine for everything ahead:

- A **Nerf dart flying through the air** — same loop, but the acceleration also includes air resistance pushing back. (No formula exists for that — only the loop.)
- A **spring pushing a dart down the barrel** — same loop, but the acceleration comes from the spring's force, which *fades* as it uncoils.
- **Compressed air** — same loop, but the push comes from gas pressure dropping as it expands.
