## Phase 2: Spells & Choices

## The problem: copy-paste is a trap

Suppose your picture needs a few empty rows — rows of five blank squares:

```python
["", "", "", "", ""]
```

You need one at the top, one in the middle, one at the bottom. So you copy-paste it three times. Fine... until you realize you wanted **six** squares per row, not five. Now you have to fix it in *every copy*, and if you miss one, your picture comes out crooked. 😬

Copy-paste feels fast but it makes work for future-you.

---

## The big idea: a function is a reusable spell

A **function** is a recipe you write once and give a name. Then you **call** it — say its name with `()` — and it does its thing. You've been writing functions since Lesson 1. The new trick today: you can **call your own function from inside another one.**

Write the blank row *once*, as its own spell:

```python
def blank_row():
    return ["", "", "", "", ""]
```

Now, anywhere you need that row, you just **call** `blank_row()`:

```python
def face():
    eyes  = ["", "black", "", "black", ""]
    smile = ["", "black", "black", "black", ""]
    return [blank_row(), eyes, blank_row(), ..., smile]
```

See how `blank_row()` appears twice, but you only *wrote* it once? If you ever want six squares instead of five, you change it in **one** place — inside `blank_row` — and every face fixes itself. That's why functions exist: **name it once, reuse it everywhere.**

---

## Calling vs. defining

- `def face():` — this **defines** the spell (writes the recipe).
- `face()` — this **calls** the spell (actually casts it).

The Graph panel calls your `face()` for you every time you press Run. Cast away. 🪄

## What you'll build

- **`blank_row()`** — a tiny spell that returns one empty row.
- **`face()`** — a 5x5 smiley that **calls** `blank_row()` for its empty rows.
