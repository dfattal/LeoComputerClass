# Lesson 8 — The Big Picture: Where Pixels Came From

## A look back at everything you made

## You've been a wizard all along

Look at what you can do now, {{FIRST_NAME}}. You started by making **one red square** appear. Then you named colors in boxes, used numbers to place blocks, wrote magic spells (functions), taught the computer to make choices, and looped to fill a whole grid. Every picture you drew was really just a **grid of tiny squares**, each one told what color to be.

That idea — *a picture is a grid of colored squares* — is one of the biggest inventions in the whole history of screens. Let's find out where it came from.

---

## The word "pixel" is only about 60 years old

The little squares are called **pixels**. The word is a squished-together nickname for **"picture element"** — the smallest *element* (piece) of a *picture*. Engineers started using it in the 1960s when they were figuring out how to send pictures back from space cameras. So the word is younger than your grandparents!

---

## The problem: how do you send a picture through a wire?

Here's the puzzle people had to solve. A photograph is a smooth thing — colors blend softly into each other. But a wire, or a computer, can only carry **numbers**. You can't squeeze a soft, smooth photo down a wire. So how do you send a picture to a TV, or to a computer, or all the way back from a spacecraft near Mars?

The clever answer was the same trick you've been using all class:

> **Chop the picture into a grid of tiny squares. Give each square one color, written as a number. Now the picture *is* a list of numbers — and numbers travel anywhere.**

When the numbers arrive, the screen just colors in the grid, square by square, and *poof* — the picture appears again. Your eyes are too far away to see the tiny squares, so it all looks smooth.

---

## Spacecraft, and a picture colored in by hand

In 1965, a little spacecraft called **Mariner 4** flew past Mars and took the first close-up photos of another planet. But the picture came back as a long list of numbers, very slowly. The scientists were too excited to wait for the computer to print it. So they bought **colored chalk**, taped strips of paper into a grid on the wall, and **colored in each square by hand** using the numbers — like a giant paint-by-numbers. That hand-colored grid was the first picture of Mars anyone had ever seen. It was made of pixels, exactly like yours.

---

## From big blocky squares to invisible ones

The first computer screens had **big** pixels — you could see every chunky square, like an 8-bit video game. As screens got better, the squares got smaller and smaller. The screen you're reading this on has *millions* of pixels, each one too tiny to see. But zoom in close enough on **any** screen — your tablet, a phone, a giant stadium scoreboard — and you'll find the same thing you drew: **a grid of colored squares.**

Every photo, every game, every video, every emoji is a grid of pixels with a color number in each square. You didn't just learn to draw with code. You learned **the secret that every screen in the world is built on.**
