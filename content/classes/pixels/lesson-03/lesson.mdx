# Numbers & Counting

> What if you want a bar that's 20 squares long? Typing `"green"` twenty times is nobody's idea of fun. Let the computer count instead. 🔢

## Phase 1: First Pictures

---

## The problem: counting by hand is slow

Say you want a row of green squares. Easy enough for three:

```python
return [["green", "green", "green"]]
```

But what if you want **twenty**? Or you want to make it "a little longer" tomorrow? You'd be typing `"green"` over and over and *counting with your finger* to get it right. Computers are supposed to do the counting for us!

---

## The big idea: numbers you can compute with

Python knows math. And one of its coolest tricks works on lists: the `*` symbol means **"make this many copies."**

```python
["green"] * 3      # -> ["green", "green", "green"]
```

Read it as: *"a list with `"green"` in it, copied 3 times."* Change the 3, change how many squares you get — no counting on your fingers.

So a bar of any length is just:

```python
def bar(n):
    return [["green"] * n]
```

Now `bar(3)` is three squares and `bar(20)` is twenty squares, from the **exact same code**. The number `n` does all the work. 💪

---

## Joining lists with `+`

You can also glue two lists together with `+`:

```python
[""] * 2 + ["red"]     # -> ["", "", "red"]
```

That's two empty squares, then a red one. The `+` puts the second list right after the first. Handy for putting something at the *end* of a gap.
