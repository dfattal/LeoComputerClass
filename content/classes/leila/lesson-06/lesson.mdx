# Mutations: Typos in the Code of Life

You know how sometimes you're texting a friend and you make a typo? Maybe you type "I like digs" instead of "I like dogs." One little change, and the meaning shifts!

**Mutations** work the same way in DNA. A mutation is any change to the letters in a DNA sequence. Sometimes the change matters a lot, and sometimes it doesn't change anything important at all.

## Three Types of Mutations

There are three main ways DNA can get "typos":

### 1. Substitution (Swap a Letter)
One base gets replaced with a different one. It's like changing "CAT" to "CAR" — just one letter is different.

> `ATCG` → change position 1 → `AGCG`

### 2. Insertion (Add a Letter)
A brand new base gets squeezed into the sequence. The DNA gets one letter longer! It's like turning "CAT" into "CLAT" — everything after the new letter shifts over.

> `ATCG` → insert T at position 2 → `ATTCG`

### 3. Deletion (Remove a Letter)
A base gets removed entirely. The DNA gets one letter shorter. It's like turning "CAT" into "AT" — the first letter just disappears.

> `ATCG` → delete position 0 → `TCG`

## Why Do Mutations Matter?

Mutations are actually super important! They are the engine of **evolution**. Every living thing on Earth — from tiny bacteria to blue whales — exists because of mutations that happened over millions of years.

Most mutations don't do anything noticeable. Some are harmful. But every once in a while, a mutation gives a creature a **new advantage** — like thicker fur to survive cold weather, or longer legs to run faster.

Without mutations, life would never change. There would be no variety, no new species, no evolution. Pretty important typos, right?

## Your Mission

Write a function that can perform all three types of mutations on a DNA string. You'll use **string slicing** to cut the DNA apart and put it back together with the change.
