## You became a genetic engineer

Look at your lab skills now, {{FIRST_NAME}}. You counted DNA bases, paired them up, read the reverse strand, transcribed DNA into RNA, translated RNA into proteins, made mutations, decoded a creature's traits from its genome, and finally used **CRISPR** to find a target in DNA and replace it — real gene editing.

Behind all of that is a 150-year story of humans slowly learning the secret of how living things pass on their traits — and finally learning to **edit** that secret. Here's how it happened.

---

## Step 1 — A monk and his pea plants (1860s)

It started in a garden. A monk named **Gregor Mendel** spent years growing **pea plants** — thousands of them — and carefully writing down what happened when he bred tall ones with short ones, or green peas with yellow. Everyone else thought traits just "blended" like mixing paint. Mendel's careful counting showed something different: traits are passed down in tidy, predictable **units**, one from each parent. He had discovered **genes** — without ever seeing one. People ignored his work for 35 years before realizing he'd found the rulebook of inheritance.

---

## Step 2 — Finding the molecule and its shape (1950s)

Mendel knew traits were passed in units, but *what were they made of?* The hunt led to **DNA**, and in **1953**, **Rosalind Franklin**, **James Watson**, and **Francis Crick** revealed its shape: a **double helix**, a twisted ladder. The rungs are the bases **A, T, C, G**, pairing A-with-T and C-with-G — the exact rule you used to build complementary strands. Suddenly genes had a *physical form*: a long message written in four letters.

---

## Step 3 — Reading the code (1960s)

Next came the question you answered yourself: *what do the letters spell?* Scientists discovered the cell reads DNA **three letters at a time**, and each three-letter word (a **codon**) tells it which amino acid to add to build a **protein** — exactly what you did when you translated RNA into protein. Proteins are the tiny machines that actually build and run your body. So the chain is: **DNA → RNA → protein → you.**

---

## Step 4 — Learning to *edit* it (2012)

For a long time we could only *read* DNA. Then, in 2012, two scientists — **Jennifer Doudna** and **Emmanuelle Charpentier** — turned a trick that bacteria use to fight viruses into a tool that can **find an exact spot in DNA and change it.** It's called **CRISPR**, and it works like the find-and-replace you used: locate a target sequence, snip it, and swap in something new. It was such a giant leap that Doudna and Charpentier won the **Nobel Prize** for it in 2020. Today scientists use CRISPR to study diseases and even to help cure some of them.

> **You went on the whole journey.** From Mendel counting peas, to Franklin's photograph, to cracking the three-letter code, to editing genes with CRISPR — that's 150 years of discovery, and you rebuilt each piece in code.

---

## Why changing one letter can change a creature

Here's the idea that ties it all together — and it's exactly what you saw when you made mutations:

1. A creature's traits come from its **proteins** (fur color, eye shape, and so on).
2. Each protein is built by **reading DNA letters in order**, three at a time.
3. If you **change one letter**, you can change one of those three-letter words…
4. …which can change **which amino acid** gets used, which can change the **protein**, which can change the **trait.**

One tiny letter, near the start of the chain, can ripple all the way out to something you can *see* — like turning a brown-furred creature white. Sometimes a one-letter change does nothing; sometimes it changes everything. That's the power, and the responsibility, of understanding the code of life.
