# RNA to Protein: Translation

## From Instructions to Action

Last lesson, you learned how cells copy DNA into RNA. But RNA is still just *instructions* — a message written in a code. Now it's time for the cell to actually **read** that message and build something with it!

This process is called **translation** — turning the RNA language into the protein language.

## Codons: Three-Letter Words

Here's the cool part: the cell reads RNA **three letters at a time**. Each group of three letters is called a **codon**.

Think of it like this:
- RNA is a **sentence**
- Codons are **words** (always exactly 3 letters long)
- Each word has a **meaning** — an amino acid

```
RNA:     A U G U U U G C U U A A
Codons:  AUG | UUU | GCU | UAA
Meaning:  M  |  F  |  A  | STOP!
```

## Amino Acids: Building Blocks of Proteins

Each codon tells the cell to add one **amino acid** to a growing chain. Amino acids are like LEGO bricks — snap enough of them together and you get a **protein**!

Proteins do almost everything in your body:
- **Muscles** are made of protein
- **Enzymes** that digest your food are proteins
- **Antibodies** that fight germs are proteins
- Even your **hair** and **fingernails** are mostly protein!

## The Start and Stop Signals

Two special rules:

1. **AUG = START** (and also codes for methionine, "M") — this codon says "begin reading here!"
2. **UAA, UAG, UGA = STOP** — these codons say "we're done, stop building!"

When the cell hits a stop codon, it releases the finished protein.

## A Simplified Codon Table

Here are some important codons to know:

| Codon(s) | Amino Acid | Letter |
|----------|-----------|--------|
| AUG | Methionine (START) | M |
| UUU, UUC | Phenylalanine | F |
| UUA, UUG | Leucine | L |
| GCU, GCC, GCA, GCG | Alanine | A |
| GAU, GAC | Aspartic acid | D |
| UAA, UAG, UGA | **STOP** | * |

Notice that some amino acids have multiple codons? That's like having different spellings for the same word. Nature built in backup options!
