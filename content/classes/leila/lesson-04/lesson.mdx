# DNA to RNA: Transcription

## The Master Cookbook

Remember how DNA stores all the instructions for building YOU? Well, here's the thing — your DNA is **super important**, so your cells keep it locked away safely inside the **nucleus** (the cell's control center).

But if the instructions are locked in a vault, how does the cell actually *use* them?

## Making a Copy

Imagine you have a **master cookbook** that's so rare and valuable, it can never leave the vault. But you need to cook dinner! What do you do?

You **copy the recipe** and take the copy to the kitchen!

That's exactly what your cells do. They make a copy of the DNA instructions called **RNA** (ribonucleic acid). This process is called **transcription** — which literally means "to copy down."

## RNA: The Mobile Copy

RNA is almost identical to DNA, with one small twist:

- DNA uses the letters: **A, T, C, G**
- RNA uses the letters: **A, U, C, G**

See the difference? RNA swaps out **T (thymine)** for **U (uracil)**. Everything else stays the same!

So when a cell transcribes DNA to RNA:
- **A** stays **A**
- **T** becomes **U** (this is the big change!)
- **C** stays **C**
- **G** stays **G**

## A Quick Example

```
DNA:  G A T T A C A
RNA:  G A U U A C A
```

Every T flipped to a U — that's it! Pretty simple, right?

## Why Bother With a Copy?

Great question! Here's why:

1. **Protection** — DNA is the original. If it gets damaged, you're in trouble. Keeping it safe in the nucleus is smart.
2. **Mobility** — RNA is small and can travel out of the nucleus to the **ribosomes** (the cell's protein-building machines).
3. **Many copies** — The cell can make tons of RNA copies from one piece of DNA, like printing lots of copies of a recipe for all your friends.
